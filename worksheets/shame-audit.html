The Shame Audit

Shame survives by staying vague and unexamined. This worksheet makes it specific — and specific things can be looked at. You don't have to feel better after this. You just have to see more clearly.

Date
Session / Solo
Before you begin

This isn't a feelings journal. It's a forensic exercise. You're gathering evidence — not performing insight, not trying to feel better on cue. Go slow. If a section feels too much, mark it and come back. Your answers auto-save to this browser.

1 Name the belief

Shame beliefs almost always take the form "I am ___" or "There is something wrong with me because ___." Write the one that feels most present for you right now — the one underneath other feelings, not the one that sounds most reasonable.

How long have you been carrying this belief? When did it first feel true?

2 Find the source

Shame is taught, not innate. Someone — or a pattern of experiences — communicated that this thing about you was a problem. Who or what taught you this belief? Be specific. This is not about blame. It's about tracing the wire back to where it was connected.

Who taught it
How it was communicated

Was the source reliable? Did they have the full picture of who you were? Were they dealing with their own unprocessed pain when they communicated this?

3 Cross-examine it

A belief is not a fact. Apply the same scrutiny to this one that you'd apply to anything else that claimed to be true.

Evidence that seems to support it
Evidence that contradicts it — moments it wasn't true

If a person you love told you they believed this exact thing about themselves, what would you say to them?

Notice the gap between what you'd say to them and what you say to yourself. That gap is worth sitting with.

4 Find it in the body

Shame has a physical signature. Bringing it to mind — even briefly — tends to produce a recognizable physical response. Right now, as you hold the belief from Part 1 in mind for a moment: where do you feel it?

Where in your body
What posture does it create

Noticing the body sensation without acting on it — without hiding, without leaving the room — is itself a small act of staying with something that usually drives you away.

5 A more accurate statement

Not a positive affirmation. Not a denial that anything happened. Just a more complete, more accurate version of the belief — one that acknowledges what's true without making it permanent or total. It might start with: "Something painful happened, and I concluded..." or "I was taught that... but what's also true is..."

Closing note
You cannot think your way out of shame — insight alone doesn't rewire what was wired through experience. But naming it, tracing it, and staying with it rather than hiding from it is where the work actually begins. If this surfaced something significant, it's worth bringing to a session.
