The Prediction Log

Anxious thoughts are predictions, not facts. This worksheet helps you track yours over time — and collect your own evidence about how accurate the alarm actually is. Do this consistently for 2–4 weeks to see the pattern.

How this works

Step 1: Before the thing happens — write the trigger, your prediction, and how certain you feel (0–100%). Step 2: After the thing happens — write what actually happened and score your prediction's accuracy. Over time, you'll build a personal track record that's more reliable than the alarm.

→ This week's main anxiety theme

Before logging individual predictions, name the general territory your anxiety has been occupying this week. Not every worry — just the main channel it keeps returning to.

3 The thought underneath the prediction

Sometimes the anxious prediction is covering a deeper feeling — something it would be more uncomfortable to name directly. Looking at your most intense predictions this week: what might be underneath them? Not the anxious thought — the feeling the thought is trying to manage.

What to do with this
The goal is not to talk yourself out of anxiety using this data. It's to build a relationship with your own track record. Over time, "my mind predicted X, but X almost never actually happens" becomes something you know from experience — not just something you've been told. That kind of knowing is different.
