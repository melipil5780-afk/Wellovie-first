Finding the Floor

If you've spent time in relationships adjusting, deferring, and editing yourself down, this worksheet helps you find what's underneath — the preferences, reactions, and wants that belong to you. You don't have to show anyone else what you write here.

Date
Session / Solo
How to use this

Work through each section slowly. Don't edit for palatability — the editing is part of what this worksheet is designed to interrupt. If a question feels hard to answer, that difficulty is information. Write toward it, not away from it.

1 The edits you make

Think of a specific relationship where you regularly adjust what you say or do. What do you edit out before speaking? What do you change about how you'd naturally react? Be concrete — not "my opinions" but the specific kinds of things.

Things I regularly don't say, or change before saying
What I'm afraid would happen if I said them as-is
2 The agreements you don't hold

Most people who lose themselves in relationships have a list of things they outwardly agree with that they don't actually believe. These aren't lies — they're survivals. Name some of them here, in the relationship context where they happen most.

What I agree with (outwardly)
What I actually think

Look at both columns. When did you learn that holding your own view in this territory wasn't safe?

3 What you want that you haven't said

Not what you think you should want. Not what would be reasonable to want. What do you actually want — in this relationship, or from relationships in general — that you've never said out loud, or only said to yourself?

4 Three sentences that are just yours

Write three statements about yourself — your preferences, reactions, values, or opinions — that are true and that you own fully. Not explanations. Not apologies. Not "I think maybe I might..." Just true things, stated plainly. You don't have to share these with anyone.

5 One small act of self-expression

Self-abandonment is built incrementally and so is the way back. Name one small, specific thing you could say or do this week that would be a little more honest than usual. Not a confrontation. Just one degree more of yourself.

The floor
The "floor" is the interior ground — the part of you that stays yours regardless of what a relationship is asking for. You've just done some excavation. It's there. It was there before any of this. The work is remembering it often enough that it stops feeling like a stranger.
