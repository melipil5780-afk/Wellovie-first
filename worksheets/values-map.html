The Values Map

Most people have never been asked what they actually want — only what they should want. This worksheet helps you find the difference. There are no right answers here. The point is to hear your own signal more clearly.

Date
Session / Solo
Before you begin

Identity work asks you to distinguish between what you were taught to want and what you actually want. These can look identical from the outside — and feel completely different from the inside. Go slowly. If a section produces nothing, that blankness is itself information. Your answers auto-save to this browser.

1 The hollow feeling

Identity loss usually announces itself as a vague dissatisfaction — hard to name, easy to dismiss. Describe what it feels like for you. Not what caused it, just the texture of the feeling itself.

When did you first notice it? Was there a moment, or has it always been there at a low hum?

2 What you were handed

Before you could form your own preferences, others shaped them for you. List the things you were told — explicitly or implicitly — that you should want, be, or value. Include the subtle ones: what success was supposed to look like, how much space you were allowed to take up, what kind of person you were supposed to be.

What I was told to want / be
Who handed it to me

Which of these do you still carry — not because you chose them, but because they never got questioned?

3 What your own signal sounds like

Your values tend to show up not as ideas but as reactions — a pull toward something, a resistance to something else, a moment of aliveness or deadness. Think back to experiences where you felt genuinely yourself: engaged, lit up, or simply at ease. What were you doing? Who were you with?

Now the opposite: moments where something felt deeply wrong — not just uncomfortable, but like a betrayal of something in you. What was happening?

Now look at your selections. Of everything you've chosen, which three feel most essential — the ones whose absence would feel like a loss of self?

5 The gap

Look at the three values you named. Now look at your actual daily life — your choices, your commitments, where your time and energy go. Where is there alignment? Where is there a gap?

Where my life reflects my values
Where my life contradicts my values

The gap isn't a failure. It's information. What would one small move toward alignment look like — something concrete and doable?

Closing note
Identity isn't something you find — it's something you practice recognizing. The values you named today might shift over time. What matters is developing the habit of checking in with yourself rather than always looking outward for the answer.
