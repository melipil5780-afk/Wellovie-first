The Defusion Log

You are not your thoughts — but your thoughts are running the show. This worksheet helps you practice stepping outside the thought, so you can see it clearly instead of living inside it.

Date
Session / Solo
Before you begin

Defusion isn't about positive thinking or proving your thoughts wrong. It's about creating distance — moving from "I am worthless" to "I'm noticing the thought that I'm worthless." That small shift changes everything. Your answers auto-save to this browser.

1 Catch the thought

Start with a thought that's been sticky lately — one that keeps returning, that you believe even when part of you knows it might not be true. Write it exactly as it sounds in your head. Don't edit it for politeness.

2 Understand what kind of thought it is

Thoughts come in patterns. Recognizing the type can help you unhook from it faster. Which of these does your thought resemble most?

Thought type What it sounds like Does yours fit?
Prediction It's going to go wrong. They'll leave. I'll fail.
Judgment I'm too much. I'm not enough. I'm broken.
Rule I should always. I must never. I can't.
Story This is just who I am. It's always been this way.
Comparison Everyone else manages this. Why can't I?

Which type fits best? Does naming the type change how the thought feels at all?

3 Practice defusion

Defusion means stepping outside the thought so you're the one observing it, not the one living inside it. Try these three moves with the thought you wrote in Part 1. Write what you notice after each one.

Move 1 — Name it

Add a prefix: "I'm having the thought that..." or "My mind is telling me that..." Write the full sentence out.

What did you notice when you wrote it that way?

Move 2 — Thank your mind

Your mind generated this thought trying to protect you. What was it trying to protect you from? What's the fear underneath it?

Move 3 — Notice the thinker

There is a part of you that noticed this thought just now. That part is not the thought — it's the one watching it. Describe that observer for a moment. What does it feel like to be the one watching, rather than the one inside it?

4 What the thought is costing you

When you're fused with this thought — living inside it as if it's just true — how does it affect your behavior? What do you do, or stop yourself from doing, because of it?

5 After the work

Return to the thought you wrote in Part 1. Read it again. On the same 0–10 scale, how much do you believe it now?

What shifted — and what didn't? Defusion doesn't always change how much you believe a thought. Sometimes it just changes your relationship to it.

Closing note
The goal isn't to get rid of hard thoughts. It's to stop being controlled by them. You will think this thought again. When you do, you can practice catching it earlier — naming it, noticing who is doing the noticing, and choosing from that place rather than reacting from inside it.
