What brings people here

You might recognize
one of these.

Most people don’t come to therapy because something is catastrophically wrong. They come because something quiet but persistent has been making life harder than it needs to be.

🌀
Anxiety that won’t switch off
You know logically that things are fine. But your body and your mind didn’t get the memo. The worry is constant, exhausting, and you’re tired of managing it alone.
“Even on good days I’m waiting for something to go wrong.”
🪞
Not knowing who you are anymore
You’ve spent so long being who other people need you to be that you’ve lost track of what you actually want, feel, or believe. The life looks right from the outside but feels hollow.
“I feel like I’m performing a version of myself I don’t fully recognize.”
🌊
Grief that has nowhere to go
Loss doesn’t always have a name. It can be a person, a relationship, a version of your life you thought you’d have. Sometimes the world moves on before you’re ready to.
“Everyone else seems okay. I don’t understand why I’m still here.”
🔁
The same patterns, over and over
In relationships, at work, in how you talk to yourself. You can see the pattern. You just can’t seem to break out of it no matter how much you understand it intellectually.
“I know why I do this. I just can’t stop doing it.”
⚡
A big transition you didn’t expect
A move, a breakup, a career shift, becoming a parent, losing a parent. Transitions can shake the foundation of who you thought you were and leave you uncertain about what comes next.
“Everything changed and I don’t know who I am in this new version of my life.”
🤝
Losing yourself in relationships
You give, accommodate, shrink, and manage. It starts small. And then one day you realize you have no idea what you actually think or want anymore.
“I don’t know how to be in a relationship and still be myself.”
My approach

Evidence-based.
Human first.

I use techniques drawn from CBT, ACT, and emotion-focused therapy — but I don’t treat people like a set of symptoms to correct. Every session is built around you.

Collaborative, not directive
We figure it out together. I don’t arrive with a pre-built plan. I arrive with genuine curiosity about your experience, and we find the path from there.
Working with the whole pattern, not just the symptom
Anxiety is rarely just anxiety. It’s connected to your history, your relationships, your self-concept. We don’t just treat the surface — we go to the root.
Warm and direct
I’ll be honest with you in a way that feels safe, not confrontational. Therapy should feel like a conversation with someone who genuinely sees you — not a performance review.
Your pace, always
There is no timeline you’re supposed to be on. We go as deep as you’re ready to go, and not a step further until you are.

About me

I became a therapist because
I know what it feels like to need one.

I didn’t come to this work from the outside. I came to it because at a certain point in my own life, the things I thought I understood about myself stopped making sense — and I needed someone who could sit with that alongside me without rushing to fix it.

That experience changed the kind of therapist I became. I believe in warmth without softness, honesty without harshness, and the idea that real change happens when someone finally feels safe enough to stop performing and start being honest — even with themselves.

I work primarily with adults in their 20s, 30s, and 40s who are navigating anxiety, identity questions, relationship patterns, and the quiet grief of lives that didn’t go the way they planned. My clients are often high-functioning people who look fine from the outside and feel far less fine on the inside.

For first-timers

What therapy with me
actually looks like.

If you’ve never done this before — or if you have and it didn’t feel right — here’s exactly what you can expect.

01
A free 15-minute call

02
An intake session
Our first full 50-minute session. I’m listening and getting to know your world. Nothing dramatic — just a real conversation.

```
Specialty 01

Anxiety & Worry

There’s a version of anxiety that’s so woven into how you function that it feels like personality. You’ve been told you’re “a worrier.” Maybe you’ve started to believe it. You manage and push through and stay on top of everything — and it’s exhausting, and it’s never quite enough.

My approach with anxiety is not about getting rid of it — anxiety is information. It’s about learning to work with it rather than being run by it. Building the ability to tolerate uncertainty without it taking over your entire nervous system.

You might recognize this
• Difficulty switching off, even when nothing is wrong
• Replaying conversations or catastrophizing future ones
• Physical symptoms — tight chest, shallow breathing, poor sleep
• High-functioning on the outside, bracing on the inside
Specialty 02

Identity & Self-Understanding

Who you are when you’re not performing for other people. What you want when you’re not managing everyone else’s needs. What your values actually are when they stop being things you inherited. These are genuinely hard questions — and they deserve more than a self-help framework.

I work with adults in the middle of major identity shifts: career changes, relationship endings, cultural transitions, late-life questioning of choices made when they were a different person. The work is slow and worthwhile.

Often sounds like
“I don’t know who I am outside of being someone’s partner / parent / the responsible one.”
Specialty 03

Life Transitions

Transitions are disorienting even when they’re chosen. Ending a marriage, starting over in a new city, leaving a career you’ve built for decades, becoming a parent, losing a parent — these involve grief and disorientation that the people around you may not have the bandwidth to hold for long.

I provide a space where you don’t have to perform okayness. Where you can be in the middle of something — not yet through it — and that’s enough to bring.

How I work

My approach

I draw primarily from psychodynamic and relational approaches — meaning I’m interested in patterns, not just symptoms. I use CBT tools where they’re genuinely useful. I work collaboratively: you set the agenda, I bring the training and the questions.
```

About me

Dr. Sarah Marchetti,
LMHC

```

I became a therapist because I was interested in what actually moves people — not in theories of what should move them, but in the real, specific, often inconvenient ways that change happens. That curiosity has never gone away.

I’ve worked in community mental health, college counseling, and now private practice. Each setting taught me something different about who people are when they’re not performing okayness, and what it takes to build enough safety for something real to happen in a room.

My approach is warm but not vague, collaborative but not passive. I bring my training and perspective into the room. I ask questions that might be uncomfortable. I notice patterns. And I genuinely look forward to the work — not as a performance of enthusiasm, but because this kind of attention to another person’s inner life is, to me, one of the most worthwhile things there is.
```
