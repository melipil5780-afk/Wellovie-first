You Don't Know What You Want
Because Nobody Ever Asked.

Most people have spent their whole lives optimizing for what they think they should want — the job, the relationship, the version of success that makes sense to everyone else. No wonder it feels hollow. This is what it looks like to start from your actual values.

M
Meli Pil, LMHC
Licensed Mental Health Counselor, LMHC

Identity · Values · Self-knowledge

At some point — maybe in therapy, maybe in a quiet moment that caught you off guard — someone asks you what you actually want. And you realize you have no idea. Not because you're confused or indecisive, but because the question has genuinely never been the organizing principle of your life. You've been answering a different question. What should I want? What would make sense? What would make people proud?

Those are not the same question. And if you've spent long enough answering the wrong one, it can feel like the real question doesn't even have an answer anymore.

How You End Up Here

The psychologists Edward Deci and Richard Ryan spent decades studying what actually motivates people — what creates genuine engagement and satisfaction versus what creates compliance and burnout. Their core finding was straightforward: there's a fundamental difference between doing something because you genuinely want to and doing it because some external pressure — approval, obligation, fear of judgment — has gotten inside your head and started to feel like desire.

They called this introjection — the process by which external expectations become internalized so thoroughly that you no longer experience them as pressure from outside. You experience them as your own values. The person who genuinely believes they want the prestigious career because it matters to them, not because they absorbed the message early that worth requires achievement, is often the last to know the difference.

Knowing what you want is not selfishness. It is the foundation of being genuinely useful to anyone else.

— Meli Pil, LMHC

The developmental psychologist James Marcia described something he called identity foreclosure — a state where a person has committed to a set of values, roles, and goals without ever actually exploring whether they fit. They adopted the blueprint handed to them — by their family, their culture, their religion, their environment — without the crisis of questioning it. They look settled. They look decided. But the foundation is borrowed, not built. And borrowed foundations tend to crack at some point, usually around the moments that should feel most meaningful.

The Hollow Feeling Has a Name

If you've ever achieved something you worked hard for and felt strangely empty afterward, this is likely what was happening. The goal was real. The effort was real. The achievement was real. But the want underneath it was somebody else's — or a younger version of yours that was trying to solve a problem that no longer exists. Getting the thing didn't deliver what you thought it would because it was never actually pointed at something you needed.

Deci and Ryan's research consistently found that people who pursue goals for intrinsic reasons — because the work itself matters to them, because it connects to who they genuinely are — report higher wellbeing, more resilience when things are hard, and more satisfaction when they succeed. Not because they're wired differently. Because the goal is actually theirs.

The question worth asking

When you imagine having the thing you're working toward — the relationship, the role, the life — who are you imagining being pleased? If the first person who comes to mind is someone other than you, that's useful information.

What Values Actually Are

Values aren't what you think you should care about. They're not your best self's aspirations or the version of you that shows up well on paper. They're what you actually care about — which you can find by looking at what you protect, what you return to, what consistently costs you something to compromise, what makes you feel most like yourself.

The work of values clarification is not inspirational. It's forensic. You're not deciding what a good person would value. You're examining your actual behavior and reactions for evidence of what you already value, whether you've named it or not. People who say they value family but structure their entire lives around work have a values conflict — not a character flaw. The conflict is information. It tells you something about what got internalized versus what's actually true.

Starting to Find Out

1
Notice what creates genuine energy vs. obligation

Pay attention to the difference in your body between saying yes to something that lights something up and saying yes to something that just feels like the right answer. Both are yes. They feel completely different. That difference is data.

2
Look at what you envy

Envy is an uncomfortable emotion that people tend to push away. It is also one of the most accurate maps to what you actually want. When you feel a flash of envy toward someone, the question isn't what's wrong with you — it's what is it about their life that you're drawn to?

3
Ask what you'd want if no one would know

A lot of what people think they want is actually what they want to want — the version of desire that looks good, that would be approved of, that would make sense to the people whose opinions matter. Strip away the audience. What's left?

4
Let the answer be smaller than you expected

A lot of people doing this work for the first time expect to discover some grand purpose. Sometimes that happens. More often, what emerges is something quieter — a need for more time doing a specific thing, or less time doing another. A relationship quality that's been missing. A way of working that fits. The small answers are not disappointments. They're usually the real ones.

The Permission You Didn't Know You Needed

Somewhere along the way, many people absorbed the message that wanting things — for yourself, based on your own preferences, not in service of anyone else — is selfish. That needs should be earned or justified. That desire is a luxury or a distraction from more important obligations.

That message is wrong. And it is expensive. A person who doesn't know what they want cannot make real choices — only perform them. Cannot build a life that fits — only assemble one that looks like it should. Cannot show up fully for others — because they have no coherent self to show up from.

Knowing what you want is not the beginning of becoming selfish. It is the beginning of becoming real. And everything that matters — every relationship, every contribution, every decision that actually counts — gets better from there.
