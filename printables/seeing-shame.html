Seeing Shame Clearly

Shame says you are the problem — not what you did, but who you are. This worksheet separates what happened from what you concluded about yourself, and asks whether that conclusion was ever actually true.

Date
Before you start

Shame work can be intense. You don't need to push through if it becomes too much — it's okay to put this down and come back. If you're carrying deep shame about something traumatic, this worksheet is most useful alongside a therapist who can be present with you.

1

Shame or guilt — which is this?

These feel similar but are fundamentally different. Knowing which you're working with changes how you approach it.

Shame — "I am bad"
  • —About who you are, not what you did
  • —Wants to hide, disappear, or shut down
  • —Makes you feel fundamentally flawed or broken
  • —Hard to speak — exposure feels dangerous
  • —Message: "I am wrong." "I am too much." "I am not enough."
Guilt — "I did something bad"
  • —About a specific action or choice
  • —Wants to repair, apologize, or make amends
  • —Leaves your core sense of self intact
  • —Easier to name — it's about behavior
  • —Message: "I made a mistake." "I did something wrong."

3

The shame message

Shame always carries a specific belief about who you are. Complete this honestly.

The belief
Because of this, I have believed that I am  .
How long have you carried this belief?  
4

Where did this conclusion come from?

Shame beliefs don't appear from nowhere. Check anything that played a role.

5

Examine the conclusion directly

Answer each question honestly. Be as fair to yourself as you would be to someone you love.

Was the person or situation that taught you this belief actually a reliable, trustworthy source of truth about who you are?

Would you apply this conclusion equally to someone you care about who had done or experienced the same thing?

Is there any evidence — even small — that this belief is not completely or permanently true?

What would you say to a close friend or a child who came to you carrying this exact shame belief about themselves?

Step 6 — A truer statement

"I made a mistake" or "something hard happened to me" is truer than "I am fundamentally broken." Write a more accurate statement — one that holds what happened without letting it define your entire worth.

Closing reflection

Shame loses some of its power when we look at it directly. What did you notice doing this?
