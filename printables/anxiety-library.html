Stage 1
Understanding the Pattern
The observational foundation. A client who can't locate anxiety in their body can't use somatic tools. A client who can't see their maintenance cycle can't interrupt it. This stage builds the map everything else references.
6 worksheets
Somatic / CBT
Your Anxiety Signature
Body map and time-of-day grid — where and when anxiety lives. The most specific entries here become reference points throughout the entire work.
Stage 1 · 1Open →
CBT
The Maintenance Cycle
Trigger → thought → body → behaviour → consequence loop. Making visible the cycle that keeps anxiety going — the foundation for all subsequent interruption work.
Stage 1 · 2Open →
CBT / EFT
The Feeling Beneath
Surface emotion versus what's underneath it. Tracing the quieter, older feeling that anxiety tends to cover. Entry point to emotion-focused work.
Stage 1 · 3Open →
Narrative / Existential
Anxiety as Messenger
Instead of fighting anxiety, getting curious about what it might be signalling. What chapter of life it is connected to. What it has been trying to protect.
Stage 1 · 4Open →
Psychoeducation
My Anxiety Profile
Broader self-assessment across anxiety types — social, health, generalised, panic, existential — without pathologising. Helps client and therapist understand the terrain.
Stage 1 · 5Open →
Somatic — deeper
The Body Speaks First
Deep somatic body mapping — quality, movement, temperature, gesture. What posture anxiety pulls you into. What the body knows before the mind does. Best for interoceptively aware clients.
Stage 1 · 6Open →
2
Stage 2
Meeting What's Underneath
Emotion-focused and somatic work before cognitive work — EFT research is clear on this. This stage slows the work down deliberately. Listening rather than managing. Feeling before changing.
8 worksheets
Emotion-Focused
What This Feeling Needs
The core EFT move — meeting the emotion underneath anxiety with curiosity rather than management. What the feeling is signalling and what it actually needs to feel heard.
Stage 2 · 1Open →
Somatic / Focusing
The Felt Sense
Gendlin-influenced. Clearing a space, finding the body-felt quality of a situation, giving it a handle word, staying with it until it shifts. Slow, deep, wordless-first.
Stage 2 · 2Open →
Compassion-Focused
The Three Circles
Paul Gilbert's three-system model — threat, drive, soothing. Understanding why anxiety isn't a character flaw, and how to begin activating the soothing system.
Stage 2 · 3Open →
Compassion-Focused
The Compassionate Self
Building an internal compassionate presence — wisdom, strength, warmth, non-judgment. A letter from the compassionate self to the anxious self. Not reassurance — genuine presence.
Stage 2 · 4Open →
Emotion Regulation
Emotions as Information
What each emotion is for — its function, signal, and action urge. Fear, sadness, shame, anger, grief each examined. Moves from managing emotions to understanding them.
Stage 2 · 5Open →
EFT / Existential — selective
The Shame Underneath
For clients where shame is the primary engine beneath the anxiety. The difference between guilt and shame. Speaking directly to the shame. Use selectively — not routinely.
Stage 2 · 6Open →
Existential — selective
Grief and Anxiety
For clients who are grieving lost time, unlived life, or the person before anxiety. Honours the grief directly rather than treating it as a symptom to manage.
Stage 2 · 7Open →
Relational / Existential — selective
When Anxiety Feels Like Love
For clients whose anxiety is entangled with attachment — anxious parenting, anxious relationships, anxiety as a form of caring. Untangling protection from love.
Stage 2 · 8Open →
Somatic / Trauma-informed — selective
The Body Memory
Somatic memory and the body's record of difficult experiences. For clients whose anxiety has a physical-historical dimension — responses that belong to then, not now.
Stage 2 · 9Open →
3
Stage 3
Building Your Regulation Toolkit
Regulation capacity needs to be in place before exposure work. A client without reliable tools at high activation will use safety behaviours extensively in Stage 5. This stage builds the toolkit from real experience — not theory.
6 worksheets
Somatic / Psychoeducation
Your Nervous System Map
Three activation states — high, mid, low — what each feels like in this specific body, and what becomes available at each level. The map that all regulation work references.
Stage 3 · 1Open →
Somatic
Building Your Regulation Menu
Testing tools in real conditions across all three activation levels — data-driven, not aspirational. What actually works for this person, not what should work in theory.
Stage 3 · 2Open →
Somatic / Breathwork
The Breath as Anchor
The physiological sigh and breath-based tools in plain language — the mechanism explained, not just the technique. What each tool is doing and when to use it.
Stage 3 · 3Open →
Somatic / Trauma-informed
Grounding When You're Overwhelmed
Sensory and body-based anchoring for high activation states. 5-4-3-2-1 and beyond — cold water, movement, orienting. Testing which ones actually work and when.
Stage 3 · 4Open →
Somatic
Movement and Anxiety
The relationship between physical movement and anxiety regulation — not as exercise prescription but as discharge. What kinds of movement work for this person specifically.
Stage 3 · 5Open →
Somatic / Existential
Rest as Radical Act
For the client whose nervous system never fully comes down. The difference between rest and collapse, rest and avoidance. What genuine restoration feels like and costs.
Stage 3 · 6Open →
4
Stage 4
Working With Thoughts
With emotional and somatic grounding now in place, cognitive work becomes accessible rather than purely intellectual. The thought log here builds the cognitive fingerprint referenced in every subsequent session.
5 worksheets
CBT
Thought Patterns Log
Building the cognitive fingerprint — identifying distortion types in real time, fusion scores, what the thought predicts and what actually happens. Referenced throughout the work.
Stage 4 · 1Open →
ACT / Defusion
Thoughts Are Weather
Stepping back from thoughts rather than being inside them. Labelling, leaves on a stream, sing-song. The goal is not to change thoughts but to change your relationship with them.
Stage 4 · 2Open →
Narrative
The Story My Mind Tells
Narrative externalisation — naming the anxious story, giving it a character, separating the person from the problem. Particularly useful for clients highly identified with their anxiety.
Stage 4 · 3Open →
ACT — pragmatic
Is This Thought Useful?
Not "is this true" but "is this helping me move toward what matters." Better for clients who have already done CBT and found thought-challenging unsatisfying or unconvincing.
Stage 4 · 4Open →
CBT / ACT
The Critic and the Witness
Dual transcript — the anxious mind voice versus the observing self without reassurance. Witnessing without fixing. Works best after some defusion capacity is in place.
Stage 4 · 5Open →
CBT / ACT — when defusion isn't enough
Writing Back to the Thought
A structured written dialogue with the most persistent anxious thought. Acknowledges what it gets right, challenges what it gets wrong, and clarifies what you choose anyway.
Stage 4 · 6Open →
5
Stage 5
Facing What You've Been Avoiding
With regulation capacity and defusion skills in place, exposure work generates cleaner data. The Cost Audit is motivational preparation — do not skip it. Right-sizing the first experiment is the most important clinical decision in this stage.
6 worksheets
Motivational
The Cost Audit
Specific costs across domains — what avoidance has taken from relationships, work, health, daily freedom. Motivational preparation before exposure work begins. Do not skip.
Stage 5 · 1Open →
Exposure / ACT
Opening the Door
Designing the first willingness experiment — right-sized, safety behaviours explicitly excluded, prediction recorded before. The most important design decision in Stage 5.
Stage 5 · 2Open →
Exposure
What Actually Happened
Recording experiment outcomes before the mind edits them. The gap between prediction and reality. Recorded immediately after the experiment — not later.
Stage 5 · 3Open →
ACT / Exposure
Living Without Guarantees
Uncertainty profile and graduated exposure hierarchy. Often needs several weeks. The question is not "can I handle certainty" but "can I act while uncertain."
Stage 5 · 4Open →
CBT
The Depths of What-If
Following the worry chain all the way to the core fear — not stopping at the first answer. Used for situational anxiety where the core fear is concrete rather than existential.
Stage 5 · 5Open →
Existential
What I'm Really Afraid Of
Existential core fear categories — death, meaninglessness, isolation, freedom and responsibility. Alternative to the worry chain for clients whose anxiety is ontological rather than situational.
Stage 5 · 6Open →

Stage 6
Emotion Regulation Skills
Concrete skills for staying present with difficult emotions. The Worry Window is the most structurally impactful single worksheet in this library for generalised anxiety. When Nothing Helps is clinically important not to skip for clients who idealise the toolkit.
6 worksheets
DBT-informed
Riding the Wave
Distress tolerance and urge surfing. Emotions as waves that peak and pass. The skill of staying present with intensity without acting on it. For high-reactivity presentations.
Stage 6 · 1Open →
DBT-informed
Opposite Action
When the emotion's action urge is making things worse. What opposite action looks like for anxiety, shame, sadness, anger — practical and specific to this client's patterns.
Stage 6 · 2Open →
CBT / Containment
The Worry Window
Postponement log and nightly offload. The most structurally impactful worksheet in the library for generalised anxiety. Creates a containment structure that makes space in the rest of the day.
Stage 6 · 3Open →
CBT / Mindfulness
Working With Rumination
The difference between problem-solving and rumination. Recognising the loop. Redirecting without suppressing. For clients whose anxiety lives primarily in their head.
Stage 6 · 4Open →
Compassion-Focused
Self-Compassion in Hard Moments
The three CFT components — mindfulness of suffering, common humanity, self-kindness — made practical. A concrete exercise for when things feel genuinely unbearable.
Stage 6 · 5Open →
Acceptance-based
When Nothing Helps
For the session after the tools fail. Radical acceptance. The difference between pain and suffering. Clinically important not to skip — clients who idealise the toolkit need this.
Stage 6 · 6Open →
Relapse Prevention / DBT-informed
Planning for Hard Days
A concrete plan for the days when everything is harder — identifying early signs, what helps most, what to reduce, and what to remember. Built when calm, used when not.

Stage 7
Who You Are and What Matters
The deepest stage — and the one most often skipped in anxiety treatment. Clients cannot do real values work while in survival mode. A Letter to the Anxiety and the logotherapy work are the most integrative documents in the library.
7 worksheets
Logotherapy
What Makes Life Feel Meaningful
Frankl's three sources — contribution, experience, attitude toward suffering. Where meaning currently lives and where it's absent. What the client wants their life to have meant.
Stage 7 · 1Open →
ACT / Values
Your Values Compass
Mapping values across domains. The intersection insight: where anxiety is loudest is where values are most alive. Three core values and where anxiety has been making decisions instead.
Stage 7 · 2Open →
CFT / Self-compassion
The Critical Inner Voice
Dual column, origin of the critic, what it actually needs underneath the harshness. A letter to the critic — not dismissing it, but speaking to it honestly. Read aloud in session.
Stage 7 · 3Open →
Narrative / Identity
Who Am I Beyond Anxiety?
Who were you before anxiety became central. What it has taken credit for that is genuinely yours. The person you are becoming and one act of reclamation this week.
Stage 7 · 4Open →
ACT / Forward-facing
The Life I Want to Build
What Monday looks like when anxiety isn't making the decisions. Six-month concrete picture. What the client must be willing to tolerate to have that life. The next specific step.
Stage 7 · 5Open →
CFT / Existential
Forgiveness and Letting Go
What the client is still carrying — anger at others, anger at self, grief about lost years. What holding on does for them. What becomes available if they put some of it down.
Stage 7 · 6Open →
Narrative — integrative
A Letter to the Anxiety
Writing directly to anxiety as an entity. What it has taken, what you understand now, what the relationship will be going forward. The most integrative document in the library. Read aloud in session.
Stage 7 · 7Open →
Compassion-Focused Therapy
A Compassionate Letter to Yourself
Writing the letter a genuinely compassionate friend would write — seeing the struggle clearly and responding with warmth. Designed to be read aloud in session.

Carrying Forward

Integration
What Has Changed
Honest before-and-after across six domains. Not whether anxiety is gone but who the client is now in relation to it. What the work cost and what it gave. Specific, evidenced, not aspirational.
Closing · 1Open →
Integration — reference
My Toolkit
Personal reference card built from actual experience — regulation tools by activation level, early warning signals, core values, people and places that help. Something to carry and update.
Closing · 2Open →
Narrative — integrative
A Letter to My Week 1 Self
The most emotionally significant document in the library. Written to the version of yourself that arrived at this work, carrying everything they were carrying. Read aloud in session.
