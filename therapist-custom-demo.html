Trauma & PTSD

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder is the nervous system’s response to experiencing or witnessing something overwhelming. It is not a character flaw, weakness, or sign that you’re broken. It is a natural response to an unnatural situation.

PTSD can follow a single traumatic event — an accident, an assault, a medical emergency, a sudden loss — or a period of sustained stress or danger. The event doesn’t have to have been life-threatening to your body. If it overwhelmed your nervous system’s ability to integrate what happened, PTSD can result.

The core difficulty in PTSD is that the memory is not filed as past. Your brain hasn’t been able to fully process and store it with a timestamp. So it arrives in the present — as flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive images — as if it’s happening again.

How I approach PTSD
  • Phase one is always stabilization — building your window of tolerance before approaching trauma material
  • EMDR is typically the primary processing modality for single-incident trauma
  • Somatic techniques to work with physical hyperarousal and body-held trauma
  • Psychoeducation about the nervous system — understanding what’s happening often reduces shame significantly
  • Gradual, titrated exposure to traumatic material — never flooding
“PTSD is not a disorder of the past. It is a disorder of the present — a nervous system that cannot locate the traumatic event safely in time. The goal is to give the memory a timestamp.”

Complex Trauma & C-PTSD

Complex PTSD arises not from a single event but from prolonged, repeated trauma — often in a context where escape felt impossible. This most commonly includes childhood abuse or neglect, domestic violence, prolonged medical trauma, or captivity.

C-PTSD includes the core features of PTSD plus additional layers: profound difficulties with emotional regulation, deep disturbances in self-perception, and persistent problems with relationships. People with C-PTSD often describe feeling fundamentally defective, or believing they are to blame for what happened to them.

The wounds of complex trauma typically require a longer, more relational treatment. The therapeutic relationship itself becomes part of the healing — experiencing safety with another person, often for the first time.

How I approach C-PTSD
  • Extended stabilization phase — often much longer than single-incident PTSD
  • Internal Family Systems (IFS) to work with the protective and exiled parts of self
  • Attachment-focused work to address relational trauma and build the capacity for safety with others
  • EMDR adapted for complex presentations — different protocol than standard PTSD
  • Careful, explicit attention to the therapeutic relationship as a healing agent in itself
“Complex trauma often produces a self that was built to survive rather than to live. The work is rebuilding — carefully, slowly, with great respect for how intelligent those survival strategies were.”

Somatic & Body-Based Approaches

Trauma is not only stored in memory and thought. It is stored in the body: in muscle tension, in breathing patterns, in chronic pain, in the disconnect between your head and your physical experience.

Many people arrive having done significant cognitive work — they understand their trauma intellectually, they can trace the patterns, they know why they react the way they do — and yet something hasn’t shifted. That is often because the work needs to go into the body.

Somatic therapy is about learning to notice, tolerate, and work with physical sensations — and ultimately to discharge the stress energy that has been held in the nervous system, sometimes for decades.

Somatic methods I use
  • Somatic Experiencing — working with the felt sense, tracking sensation, titrated discharge
  • Grounding and orienting practices to support nervous system regulation
  • Body-based EMDR protocols that incorporate somatic processing
  • Breathwork and nervous system regulation techniques
  • Working with the Window of Tolerance to gradually expand capacity
“The body is not a container for the mind. It is the place where the mind was formed, and where much of the healing has to happen.”

Childhood Trauma & Family Wounds

Childhood trauma doesn’t require dramatic events. Emotional neglect — not being seen, not having your feelings acknowledged, growing up in a home where love was conditional or unpredictable — can shape the nervous system just as profoundly as overt abuse.

Adults who grew up in these environments often arrive having spent years convinced that they are the problem. Too sensitive, too needy, too much, not enough. One of the most important early pieces of work is simply being believed — having someone witness what your childhood was actually like, without minimizing it.

The long reach of childhood wounding shows up in how you see yourself, how you attach to others, how safe you feel in your own body, and what you believe you deserve. Healing means updating those early conclusions — carefully, with great compassion for the child who formed them.

How I work with childhood trauma
  • IFS (Internal Family Systems) to work with child parts and protectors with gentleness
  • Attachment-focused therapy to understand and gradually update relational patterns
  • EMDR for early memories that are held as body-based and pre-verbal
  • Working with the inner critic and shame-based self-beliefs directly
  • Reparative relational experiences within the therapeutic relationship itself
“The child who needed to adapt to survive was very intelligent. The work is not to shame those adaptations — it’s to help the adult you are now realize you have more choices than you think.”

Trauma

Your Nervous System Is Not Broken. It Did Exactly What It Was Built to Do.

By Dr. Elena Vasquez, LCSW  ·  8 min read

One of the first things I say to almost every new client who comes in with trauma is this: what your nervous system did makes complete sense. It is not a malfunction. It is not evidence that you are weak, broken, or defective. It is evidence that your brain and body did exactly what millions of years of evolution built them to do.

The problem is not that the system failed. The problem is that the system is still running a threat response that was written for a situation that no longer exists.

What your nervous system was trying to do

When you experience something overwhelming — something your brain cannot fully process in real time — it activates your survival system. Heart rate up. Muscles primed. Attention narrowed to the threat. Time slows down. The brain records everything with extraordinary intensity.

This is designed to help you survive. And in the moment, it works.

The problem comes later. Under normal circumstances, after a threat has passed, the nervous system is supposed to discharge the activation — shake it out, so to speak — and return to baseline. The memory gets filed with a timestamp: this happened, and it is over.

Trauma is what happens when this filing process is interrupted. The memory stays active, unprocessed, without a timestamp — as if it’s still happening.

Why your reactions make sense

This is why trauma survivors often feel confused by their own reactions. You know logically that you are safe. You know the event is over. And yet your body doesn’t seem to have received the memo. You startle at sounds that don’t bother other people. You go numb in situations that others find warm and inviting. You feel a surge of panic in circumstances that bear only a passing resemblance to the original threat.

This is not irrationality. This is a nervous system that learned, under genuine threat, to scan for danger at all times — and then never got the signal that it was safe to stop.

What healing actually involves

Understanding this changes what healing looks like. The goal is not to talk yourself out of your reactions. It is not to override the nervous system with willpower or logic. It is to help the nervous system complete the processing it couldn’t complete at the time — to give the memory a timestamp, so it can be stored as past rather than present.

This is why approaches like EMDR and somatic therapy work differently from traditional talk therapy. They work with the nervous system directly, not just with the narrative of what happened. They address the place where the trauma actually lives.

You are not broken. Your nervous system is doing what it was designed to do. And what it was designed to do, it can also be helped to update.

C-PTSD

The Difference Between PTSD and C-PTSD — And Why It Matters for Your Healing

By Dr. Elena Vasquez, LCSW  ·  10 min read

Two people can both have trauma histories and need completely different paths to heal. One of the most important distinctions in trauma work — and one that is still widely misunderstood outside of specialized practice — is the difference between PTSD and Complex PTSD.

Getting this distinction right matters. Because treating complex trauma with a PTSD model is one of the reasons people spend years in therapy without feeling like the thing has shifted.

What standard PTSD looks like

Classic PTSD typically follows a discrete traumatic event. A car accident. An assault. A medical emergency. A disaster. The traumatic experience has a beginning and an end — even if processing it doesn’t. The nervous system gets stuck with an unprocessed memory of something specific.

The symptoms tend to cluster around that event: flashbacks to it, nightmares about it, avoidance of things that remind you of it, hypervigilance that looks like bracing for a repeat of it.

What makes complex trauma different

Complex PTSD (C-PTSD) arises from prolonged, repeated trauma — usually in a context where escape wasn’t possible. This most commonly means childhood abuse or neglect, domestic violence, captivity, or sustained emotional maltreatment over years.

The key difference is not just the type of event, but the developmental impact. When trauma occurs repeatedly during childhood — especially relational trauma by caregivers — it doesn’t just leave memories. It shapes the architecture of the self.

C-PTSD includes the core PTSD symptoms, but adds three additional clusters that standard PTSD doesn’t capture:

  • Emotional dysregulation: Intense reactions that feel disproportionate, or the opposite — emotional numbness and shutdown
  • Negative self-concept: Deep, pervasive shame; feeling fundamentally defective; believing on some level that you caused or deserved what happened
  • Relational difficulties: Chronic distrust, difficulty with intimacy, patterns of either avoiding connection or attaching desperately — then bracing for abandonment

Why treatment has to be different

Standard trauma treatment — including EMDR in its basic form — targets specific traumatic memories. That works well for single-incident PTSD. But with complex trauma, there often isn’t one memory to process. There are hundreds. Or the trauma is pre-verbal, or so pervasive that it didn’t produce discrete memories — it produced a worldview.

Complex trauma treatment requires a much longer stabilization phase, careful attention to the therapeutic relationship itself as a healing mechanism, and approaches that work with identity and attachment — not just memory processing.

If you’ve tried EMDR or other trauma work and felt like it didn’t reach the thing, or made you feel worse without resolution, it’s possible the approach wasn’t matched to the complexity of what you were carrying.

What EMDR Actually Feels Like (From Someone Who Has Done It Themselves)

By Dr. Elena Vasquez, LCSW  ·  9 min read

I have been in EMDR. I know what it’s like to be the client, not just the clinician. And the descriptions I read before I experienced it — mechanisms, bilateral stimulation, adaptive information processing — were accurate and also completely failed to prepare me for what it actually felt like.

So here is a more honest account.

Before you start: the preparation phase

Good EMDR doesn’t begin with processing. It begins with preparation — sometimes many sessions of it. Before we touch any traumatic material, we spend time building what is called your “window of tolerance”: your nervous system’s capacity to experience activation without tipping into overwhelm or shutdown.

This phase involves developing what are called “resources” — internal states of safety, calm, or strength that you can access when things get difficult. This isn’t filler. It is genuinely foundational. The preparation work is what makes the processing safe.

What a processing session actually feels like

During EMDR processing, you hold a specific memory lightly in mind — the image, the body sensation, the belief about yourself it activates. You don’t need to narrate it in detail. You just hold it. And then you follow the bilateral stimulation: usually eye movements tracking a therapist’s fingers, or tapping, or audio tones that alternate between ears.

What happened for me was not what I expected. I expected it to feel like reliving. It didn’t. It felt more like watching something from a distance, while it shifted. Associations came up spontaneously — other memories, physical sensations, realizations. I didn’t control any of it. The therapist periodically checked in, and we continued.

The phrase that comes closest to describing it: it felt like something that had been stuck was finally moving. Not violently. Just… releasing its grip.

After a session

EMDR processing continues after the session ends. In the days following, you might notice new memories surfacing, dreams that feel related, or emotional releases that seem to come from nowhere. This is normal. The brain is continuing to process. I ask all of my EMDR clients to keep a brief journal between sessions for exactly this reason.

Not every session produces dramatic shifts. Sometimes it’s subtle — a memory that used to feel charged that now feels quieter. Sometimes it’s more significant. Progress is not always visible in the moment.

What I can tell you is this: after EMDR, something I had been carrying for years weighed less. Not gone — but lighter, and filed somewhere I could actually access without being overwhelmed by it. That is what effective trauma treatment can do.

Why Talking About It Isn’t Always Enough — And What to Do Instead

By Dr. Elena Vasquez, LCSW  ·  7 min read

If you’ve spent time in therapy talking about your trauma — understanding your patterns, building insight, knowing why you do what you do — and you still feel like something hasn’t shifted, you are not alone. And you are not imagining it.

Understanding something cognitively is not the same as the body no longer responding to it. Trauma stored in the nervous system and muscles doesn’t update in response to insight. It updates in response to a different kind of process.

What talk therapy does well

Talk therapy is genuinely valuable. Building a trusting relationship with a therapist, developing language for your experience, gaining perspective, reducing shame, understanding your history — these are real and important. I am not dismissing them.

But the cortex — the thinking brain — is not where trauma primarily lives. Trauma lives in the subcortical brain, in the limbic system, in the body itself. And those areas don’t respond well to explanation.

What somatic work adds

Somatic approaches work with the body directly. Not through exercise or movement, but through learning to track and tolerate the physical sensations that are part of how trauma is held. Noticing where tension lives in the body. Learning to stay present with activation without tipping into overwhelm. Gradually discharging the stress energy that has been stored there, sometimes for decades.

The goal of somatic work is not catharsis — it is titration. Small, manageable doses of activation, followed by return to regulation. Over time, this expands the window of what the nervous system can tolerate — which is the foundation everything else rests on.

Emotional Neglect Is Invisible. That Doesn’t Mean It Didn’t Happen.

By Dr. Elena Vasquez, LCSW  ·  9 min read

The hardest trauma to name is the kind that never looked dramatic from the outside. No specific event. No clear scene. Just a consistent, quiet absence — of attunement, of being truly seen, of having your inner world acknowledged as real and important.

Emotional neglect is defined not by what happened, but by what didn’t. And that makes it one of the most difficult wounds to identify — and one of the most common.

Why it’s hard to name

People who grew up with emotional neglect often struggle to claim it as trauma because nothing happened. Their parents weren’t abusive. They were fed, housed, taken to school. From the outside, things looked fine.

But from the inside: their feelings weren’t asked about. Their distress wasn’t soothed. Their joy wasn’t shared. They learned very early that their inner life was not particularly interesting or important to the people who mattered most. And so they stopped bringing it forward — and eventually stopped noticing it themselves.

The legacy of emotional neglect is often not pain. It is emptiness. A vague sense that something is missing without being able to name what.

What this produces in adult life

Adults who grew up with emotional neglect often present with a confusing mix of apparently contradictory experiences. They may be highly competent and high-achieving, while simultaneously feeling hollow. They know what they think but not what they feel. They can describe their life but not inhabit it.

They often struggle to identify their own needs — not because they don’t have any, but because they learned so thoroughly to suppress them that access has been lost. They may feel numb, or chronic low-grade depression, or a persistent sense that something is wrong without being able to point to what.

How it heals

Healing from emotional neglect often begins with the therapeutic relationship itself — with the experience of being genuinely seen and curious-about by another person, perhaps for the first time. This is not abstract. It is a lived, corrective experience that gradually updates what the nervous system believes is possible.

Trauma Healing Is Not Linear. Here’s What to Expect Instead.

By Dr. Elena Vasquez, LCSW  ·  6 min read

One of the most destabilizing things about trauma recovery is the expectation that it should feel like progress all the time. That if you’re doing the work, things should keep getting better in a steady, measurable way.

They don’t. And understanding why is one of the most important things you can learn before you begin.

Why healing spirals

Trauma recovery is not a straight line. It’s a spiral. You revisit similar themes, similar triggers, similar pain points — but each time, ideally, from a slightly different vantage point. What felt overwhelming the first time through might be manageable the third time. That’s growth, even though from inside it can feel like going in circles.

There will also be periods that feel like regression — where things get harder before they get better. This is especially common after a productive processing session. The nervous system has been activated. Integration takes time and energy. Feeling worse after a breakthrough is not a sign that the work isn’t working. It is often a sign that something is moving.

What to watch for instead

Rather than asking “am I better today than yesterday?” — a question that will often discourage you — I encourage clients to ask longer questions. Not day to day, but month to month, season to season:

  • Is the recovery time after a difficult moment shorter than it used to be?
  • Are there situations that used to trigger me that now feel manageable?
  • Do I have more language for what I’m experiencing than I used to?
  • Is there more space between the trigger and my reaction, even occasionally?

These are the real measures of progress in trauma work. Not the absence of hard days, but the slow expansion of capacity and choice.
