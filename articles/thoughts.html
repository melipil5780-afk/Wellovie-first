You Are Not Your Thoughts.
But Your Thoughts Are Running the Show.

The thought feels completely true. Urgent. Real. That doesn't mean it's accurate. Here's what's actually happening when your mind turns against you — and two of the most effective tools for working with it.

M
Meli Pil, LMHC
Licensed Mental Health Counselor, LMHC
7 min read
Thoughts · CBT · Self-awareness

A thought arrives. It tells you something — about yourself, about a situation, about what someone thinks of you, about how something is going to go. It feels true. It feels obvious. It feels like it would be naive not to believe it.

And somewhere underneath, if you're honest, there's a quiet awareness that you've been here before. That this particular thought — maybe wearing different clothes, maybe about a different situation — has showed up and convinced you before. And sometimes it was right. But sometimes it wasn't. And you couldn't always tell which was which while it was happening.

That experience is not a character flaw. It's how the human mind works. And understanding the mechanics of it changes what you can do about it.

What Thoughts Actually Are

Aaron Beck, the psychiatrist who developed cognitive therapy in the 1960s, noticed something that seems obvious once it's pointed out: between a situation and how you feel about it, there's always a thought. Not always a conscious, deliberate one — often it's automatic, fast, a flash of interpretation that happens before you're even aware of it. But it's there. And it's doing most of the emotional work.

Beck catalogued the patterns these automatic thoughts tend to fall into — what he called cognitive distortions. Not random errors, but consistent, predictable ways the mind bends reality, usually in the direction of the worst-case scenario.

Common distortions
How the mind bends reality
  • All-or-nothing — it's perfect or it's a failure
  • Catastrophizing — this will definitely be the worst outcome
  • Mind reading — I know what they think of me
  • Personalization — this is about me, because of me
  • Filtering — I can only see the evidence that confirms it
  • Should statements — rigid rules about how things must be
What they have in common
They feel like facts
  • They arrive fast, before you can question them
  • They feel obvious — like anyone would think this
  • They trigger real emotions, which seem to confirm them
  • They're consistent — the same patterns appear over and over
  • They're resistant to counter-evidence
  • They've been there so long they feel like your personality

The CBT approach to these patterns is to examine them — to slow down the thought and ask whether the evidence actually supports it. What would you say to a friend who told you they believed this? Is there another explanation for the same situation? What's the realistic worst case, and could you handle it? Not to argue yourself into positivity, but to bring accuracy back into a process that has been running on automatic.

Challenging a thought doesn't mean arguing with it. It means asking whether it's actually true.

— Meli Pil, LMHC

But What If You Can't Step Outside the Thought?

Beck's approach works well for many people in many situations. But there's a problem some people run into: when you're fully fused with a thought — when it's not just a belief but feels like the ground you're standing on — trying to examine it from inside it is like trying to see your own eyes. You're too close.

This is where a different tradition, Acceptance and Commitment Therapy developed by Steven Hayes, adds something genuinely useful. Hayes noticed that the problem isn't always the content of the thought. Sometimes the problem is the relationship to the thought — the way we treat thinking as reality rather than as mental activity.

His approach, called cognitive defusion, isn't about challenging whether the thought is true. It's about creating distance from it — recognizing that you are the one noticing the thought, not the thought itself. You are not I'm a failure. You are the person who is having the thought I'm a failure. That gap, which sounds small, changes everything about what you can do next.

Two tools, different entry points

CBT says: examine the thought — is it accurate? ACT says: step back from the thought — is it useful? They're not in conflict. Beck works best when you can get some cognitive traction. Hayes works best when the thought has you so fused that examination feels impossible. Both are worth having.

What Defusion Actually Looks Like

Defusion isn't complicated, but it is counter-intuitive. Instead of engaging with the content of the thought, you observe it happening. Some people find it helps to name it explicitly: I'm having the thought that I'm going to fail. Not I'm going to fail — but I notice I'm having that thought. The difference is subtle in words and significant in experience.

You can also simply notice the thought as a mental event — something happening in your mind, like weather passing through. It arrived without being invited. It will leave without being pushed. You don't have to act on it, argue with it, or believe it. You can just watch it be there.

The Bigger Shift

Both approaches point toward the same fundamental reorientation: your thoughts are not you. They are something your mind produces — constantly, automatically, in response to everything — and they vary wildly in accuracy, usefulness, and origin. Some are wise. Some are the echoes of old fear. Some are the anxious system doing its job too aggressively. Some are genuinely useful warnings.

The skill is not learning to silence the mind. It's learning to listen to it with more discernment — to be in relationship with your thinking rather than simply inside it. That shift, practiced over time, changes what the mind can do to you. And more importantly, what it can do for you.
